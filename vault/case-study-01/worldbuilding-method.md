# Worldbuilding Method

## Purpose

Explains the general method behind building a world where quests, conflicts, and NPC behavior emerge as consequences rather than being placed on top of the setting.

## Core Principle

Cause needs consequence. An unusual idea needs a foundation. A power needs a limit. An exception needs a cost — or the audience begins to feel that anything can happen whenever the story needs it.

Story comes first. Rules should not trap the story before its emotional shape exists. Systems get built afterward, by noticing what the story keeps returning to and asking why those details matter.

## Explanation

The method used to anchor Erit's history: build the divergence around one character rather than an abstract historical event.

A major historical figure was displaced into Roman history as a child, from a far more advanced civilization. The family that found him understood his origin would make him a target if it became public, so they hid it — training him, using his survival abilities, and shaping him into an asset for war and power.

His importance isn't a plan he arrives with. It's that his presence, wound, usefulness, hidden origin, and eventual removal change how people act around him. He becomes the historical hinge specifically because Rome changes *around* his displacement, body, betrayal, and preservation as a symbol — not because he intends any of it.

The underlying chain: experience → emotion → reason → behavior → atmosphere → quest logic. Experience is the lived pressure a character, city, or culture has been through. Emotion is the state that pressure shapes. Reason is how that state makes sense to the person feeling it. Behavior is what follows. Atmosphere is what accumulates once many behaviors repeat in the same place. Quest logic appears once those behaviors create real needs, obstacles, and consequences.
