# NPC Emotional State Model

## Core Principle

NPC behavior should come from emotional history, not universal reaction rules. A character behaves a certain way because of their emotional state, and that state comes from what they lived through — modifiable by later experience, including the player's actions.

Base model: past experience → current emotional state → likely reaction → modified by new experience.

## Emotional Gravity

Characters fall easily into certain emotions because life trained them to expect certain outcomes. In the four founding siblings: Rutare (sheltered and pressured) falls into inadequacy, avoidance, quiet control. Ilunis (used and maimed) falls into anger, jealousy, entitlement, punishment. Teresta (dismissed and cast out) falls into distance, obsession, proving value, control through knowledge. Ermerus (given freedom without discipline) falls into charm, interpretation, appetite, control through meaning.

Smaller NPCs work the same way — a homeless man treated like trash falls quickly into suspicion; a guard raised in Regulatus falls quickly into duty and fear of shame.

## Context Changes Meaning

The value of an action depends on the emotional state of the person receiving it. Giving a weapon to one NPC may build trust because they feel safer. Giving the same weapon to a war-traumatized NPC may damage trust — to them it can mean you do not see me, you see a soldier, you are asking me to become the thing that broke me again.

The same object, phrase, or choice should not mean the same thing to every character. Meaning comes from emotional history.

## Layered NPC Conditions

**Location Layer** — pressure specific to the city, district, clinic, or settlement. Should not be reused everywhere, or every location feels the same.

**Group Layer** — pressure from the NPC's group, family, class, faction, or social role. Should stay specific enough to place and faction that the same role feels different in different conditions.

**Personal Layer** — the individual layer: dialogue style, background, emotional wound, trust threshold, fears, habits, memory. The only layer that can be reused as a pattern (an archetype like suspicious survivor or guilty doctor) — but the location and group layers should change how that archetype behaves.

Example. A homeless man in a wealthy district (watched by guards, treated as filth, remembers kindness) behaves differently than the same archetype in a dangerous alley (charming, opportunistic, ready to lead the player into a mugging) — same personal type, different layers, different person.

## Harsh Reality Rule

Not "poor people are good, rich people are bad." Instead: a harsh world shapes everyone. Some become kind despite suffering. Some become cruel because of it. Some switch depending on hunger, fear, and opportunity. Most people are just trying to survive the reality they were born into.

## Design Use

Before writing an NPC reaction: what location pressure shapes this person? What group pressure? What personal emotional state? What past experience makes this reaction likely? How can the player's action modify that state?
